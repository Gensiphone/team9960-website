01 / What We Do

More than a club. A workshop where students own the engineering.

№ 01

Engineering, end-to-end

Students design in CAD, machine and 3D-print parts, wire electronics, and write the autonomous code. Mentors coach. They don't build the robot.

№ 02

Compete & iterate

Each FTC season is roughly eight months of design, build, code, and competition. We treat every meet as a data point — what failed, what held up, what we'd redesign tomorrow.

№ 03

Pay it forward

We mentor younger teams, host community demos, and run STEM events. Robotics shouldn't be a city-only opportunity.

How we build.

FTC's starting envelope is 18×18×18 inches. The game changes every September. Within those constraints, our students design a fresh robot every year — drivetrain, lift, intake, vision, control, and code, all chosen to suit that season's challenge.

  • DesignOnshape · CAD-first
  • Fabrication3D printing · machining
  • DrivetrainMecanum or tank, season-dependent
  • ControlREV Control + Expansion Hub
  • VisionLimelight · AprilTag · OpenCV
  • CodeJava · FTC SDK
  • Envelope18 × 18 × 18 inches at start
  • Weight target~ 35–42 lb

01 / SEP
Kickoff & Strategy
FIRST drops the new game on a Saturday in early September. We watch the reveal as a team, read the manual cover-to-cover, and argue about scoring strategies for two weeks.
02 / OCT
Prototype
Sketches, foamboard mockups, and cardboard versions of every mechanism we're considering. Most ideas die here. That's the point.
03 / NOV
CAD & Build
Onshape models, machined and printed parts, wiring diagrams. The robot becomes real, then immediately stops working, then works again.
04 / DEC
Code & Tune
TeleOp first, then autonomous. Encoder counts, PID loops, vision pipelines — and a lot of "why is it doing that?"
05 / JAN–APR
Compete
League meets, qualifiers, and — if we earn it — championship. Drive practice. Pit repairs. Sleep optional.
